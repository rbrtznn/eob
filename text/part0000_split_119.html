TWO STREAMS OF TRADITION?

In view of the above, another theory to be suggested is that the second Cainan was removed by Jews (both before Christ and after through the Masoretes) from an ancient stream of textual tradition. This is important for Orthodox Christians who have traditionally held the LXX to be the normative Scriptural witness. This approach can take into account two elements: the first one is that the second Cainan may have been removed from the list on account of the so-called ‘Curse of Cainan.’[2904] This is hinted at in Jubilees 8:1-5, which recounts that:

Cainan found a writing which former (generations) had carved on the rock, and he read what was thereon, and he transcribed it and sinned owing to it; for it contained the teaching of the Watchers in accordance with which they used to observe the omens of the sun and moon and stars in all the signs of heaven. And he wrote it down and said nothing regarding it; for he was afraid to speak to Noah about it lest he should be angry with him on account of it.

This story would explain why Cainan would have been purged from the genealogies. However, there is more to this mystery than the presence or absence of the second Cainan, in that the genealogies presented in the MT, LXX and ST (Samaritan Text) are quite different, as documented below:

In the above table, we have the dating of the Masoretic, Samaritan, and Septuagint from Adam to the flood. Column 1 shows the ages given for when the patriarchs had fathered their sons. Column 2 shows the number of years lived after their sons were born. Column 3 shows the total of years lived by each patriarch. Column 4 shows the year since Adam’s creation each patriarch was born. Column 5 shows the year of death of each patriarch from the time Adam was created.

As can be seen by the total on the bottom line, the Masoretic Text (MT) year of the flood was 1656; the Samaritan Text (ST), 1307; the Septuagint (LXX), 2262.

The reason that the MT and ST are reduced by over 600-900 years in total from Adam to Noah is not because they dispute the total ages of the patriarchs; it is because they dispute the year in which the patriarchs fathered their sons (cf. Column 1), which becomes the issue at hand.

As can be seen by the agreement of the MT and LXX in Jared, Methuselah and Lamech, a basic pattern is agreed upon by both the MT and the LXX, and the pattern is 100% concordant. This pattern is important because it determines the total ages given in Column 4.

While there is disagreement between MT and LXX in the first 5 names, it is important to look for another method to establish accuracy when there is disagreement, and it may be suggested that the method is found here in Jared, Methuselah and Lamech. With this in mind, we note that the Septuagint follows this same pattern, the Masoretic does not.
