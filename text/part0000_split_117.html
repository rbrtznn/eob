DID THE ORIGINAL LUKE INCLUDE THE SECOND CAINAN?

The first hypothesis to be tested is that Luke did not include this second Cainan but that a very early copyist accidentally repeated ΤΟΥΚΑΙΝΑΝ (the son of Cainan), as in:

ΤΟΥΣΑΡΟΥΧΤΟΥΡΑΓΑΥΤΟΥΦΑΛΕΓΤΟΥΕΒΕΡΤΟΥΣΑΛΑ

ΤΟΥΑΡΦΑΞΑΔΤΟΥΣΗΜΤΟΥΝΩΕΤΟΥΛΑΜΕΧ

ΤΟΥΜΑΘΟΥΣΑΛΑΤΟΥΕΝΩΧΤΟΥΙΑΡΕΔΤΟΥΜΑΛΕΛΕΗΛΤΟΥΚΑΙΝΑΝ

ΤΟΥΕΝΩΣΤΟΥΣΗΘΤΟΥΑΔΑΜΤΟΥΘΕΟΥ

becoming:

ΤΟΥΣΑΡΟΥΧΤΟΥΡΑΓΑΥΤΟΥΦΑΛΕΓΤΟΥΕΒΕΡΤΟΥΣΑΛΑΤΟΥΚΑΙΝΑΝ

ΤΟΥΑΡΦΑΞΑΔΤΟΥΣΗΜΤΟΥΝΩΕΤΟΥΛΑΜΕΧ

ΤΟΥΜΑΘΟΥΣΑΛΑΤΟΥΕΝΩΧΤΟΥΙΑΡΕΔΤΟΥΜΑΛΕΛΕΗΛΤΟΥΚΑΙΝΑΝ

ΤΟΥΕΝΩΣΤΟΥΣΗΘΤΟΥΑΔΑΜΤΟΥΘΕΟΥ

In English, this becomes:

“the son of Serug, the son of Reu, the son of Peleg, the son of Eber, the son of Shelah, the son of Cainan,

the son of Arphaxad, the son of Shem, the son of Noah, the son of Lamech,

the son of Methuselah, the son of Enoch, the son of Jared, the son of Mahalalel, the son of Cainan,

the son of Enosh, the son of Seth, the son of Adam, the son of God.”

The main issue with this theory is that all MSS of Luke (with two exceptions) contain the second Cainan, which is why all English translations also include this second Cainan. The two exceptions are D (Codex Bezae) which is notoriously unreliable, especially in Luke, and P75 which does not only omit the name Cainan from Luke 3:36, but also all of Luke 3:23 to 3:33.[2901]

The second issue – the fact that our LXX manuscripts contain the second Cainan – may be overcome by suggesting that the Christian copyists of the LXX would have decided to insert the second Cainan to conform with their copies of Luke 3:36. It is impossible to tell what all the earlist pre-Christian LXX manuscripts read, but there are reasons to believe that the second Cainan has pre-Lukan origins. Jared L. Olar comments:

The Jewish historian Demetrius, who lived in the 200s B.C. around the same time that the Septuagint translation was beginning to be prepared, also included the second Cainan. The writings of Demetrius are lost, but some of his statements were preserved by Alexander Polyhistor, who lived around the time that Christ was born. Like Demetrius, Polyhistor also included the second Cainan.[2902]

The Book of the Jubilees (c. 100 BC) also includes the second Cainan, albeit with a different age for his begetting of Salah. This is significant because several fragments of ‘Jubilees’ were found among the Dead Sea Scrolls. In an attempt to explain the possible origin of the divergence between the ‘1 and 2 Cainan’ streams, Jared L. Olar suggests:

[The] second Cainan was known to Jewish writers as early as the 200s B.C., while it is only with Philo Judaeus in the early first century A.D. that we have evidence of the absence of this name from Abraham’s genealogy. What is likely to have happened is that at some point between 200 B.C. and the birth of Christ, a scribe must have accidentally skipped over the second Cainan in Gen. 11, thus creating a family or tradition of manuscript copies that left him out. In time, scribes who were confused by the second Cainan’s absence from Gen. 11 but presence in Gen. 10 and I Chron. 1 would have begun to “correct” their copies by deleting him from Gen. 10 and I Chron. 1. This would explain why the Septuagint, Demetrius, Polyhistor, and Jubilees include the second Cainan, while Philo, Josephus, Africanus, the Vulgate, and the Masoretic omit him. Because St. Luke followed the Septuagint, his genealogy includes the second Cainan.

This reasoning does not convince other scholars and apologists who support the view that the second Cainan was introduced in the LXX after 220 AD, on the basis of Josephus, Africanus,[2903] P75, and D.
